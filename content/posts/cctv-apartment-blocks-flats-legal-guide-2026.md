Essential guidance on CCTV installation in apartment blocks and flats: planning permission, communal area coverage, data protection obligations, and resident privacy rights under UK law.

<hr class="faq-divider">

## Frequently Asked Questions

<div class="faq-list">

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Do I need planning permission to install CCTV in my flat?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

For most flats, you do not need planning permission for CCTV cameras. However, if you live in a listed building or conservation area, you may need consent from your local council. Critically, if you are in a leasehold flat, you will almost certainly need permission from the freeholder or management company before installing any external cameras. Internal cameras within your own flat are your own business, but anything visible from communal areas requires approval.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Can my landlord install CCTV in communal areas?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes, landlords and management companies can install CCTV in communal areas such as hallways, lobbies, and car parks, but they must comply with UK GDPR. This means they must display clear signage, have a lawful basis for processing (usually legitimate interests), conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA), limit retention to 30 days, and respond to subject access requests within one calendar month.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Can I point my flat CCTV camera at the front door?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes, you can install a camera covering your own front door, but you must ensure it does not capture neighbouring flats' doors or communal areas excessively. Under ICO guidance, if your camera captures areas beyond your property boundary — including shared hallways — data protection law applies. You should position the camera to minimise capture of other residents' private spaces, consider using privacy masking, and display a small sign.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Is audio recording legal on apartment CCTV?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Audio recording is significantly more restricted than video under UK law. RIPA 2000 and the GDPR treat audio as more privacy-invasive. In a multi-tenant building, recording conversations in communal areas is very difficult to justify. The ICO recommends disabling audio on domestic CCTV systems unless you have a specific, documented need and clear signage.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>How long can apartment CCTV footage be kept?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

The standard retention period for CCTV footage in residential blocks is 30 days. Under UK GDPR storage limitation principles, footage must not be kept longer than necessary. If footage is needed for an ongoing police investigation or legal proceedings, it may be retained until the matter is resolved. Automatic overwrite features should be enabled to ensure routine deletion.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Can residents request to see CCTV footage of themselves?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes. Under UK GDPR Article 15, any resident who appears on CCTV has the right to make a subject access request (SAR) for a copy of their personal data. The management company must respond within one calendar month. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 introduced a 'stop the clock' provision — if more information is needed to locate the footage, the clock pauses until the requester provides it.

</div>
</details>
