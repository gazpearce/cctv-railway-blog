Expert guidance on CCTV for museums, galleries, and exhibition spaces: discreet surveillance options, heritage building compliance, and the balance between security and visitor experience.

<hr class="faq-divider">

## Frequently Asked Questions

<div class="faq-list">

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Do museums need planning permission for CCTV?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

If the museum or gallery is located in a listed building or conservation area — as many are — planning permission may be required before installing external CCTV cameras. Internal cameras typically do not require planning permission, but listed building consent may be needed if mounting cameras involves drilling into historic fabric. Always consult your local conservation officer before installation.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>What type of cameras are best for art galleries?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Discreet dome cameras are preferred in gallery environments as they are less visually intrusive than bullet cameras. Many modern galleries use mini-dome or pinhole cameras that blend into ceiling designs. For high-value exhibits, consider adding individual exhibit-level cameras with motion detection. All cameras should be fixed (not PTZ) in public gallery spaces to avoid distracting visitors.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Can museums use CCTV in special exhibition spaces?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes, and it is standard practice. Temporary exhibition spaces should have the same level of CCTV coverage as permanent galleries. Additionally, lenders of high-value artworks often require specific CCTV coverage as a condition of loan agreements. These contractual requirements should be documented and integrated into the venue's CCTV policy.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>How long should gallery CCTV footage be retained?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

The standard retention period for museum and gallery CCTV is 30 days, consistent with ICO guidance. However, if an incident occurs — theft, vandalism, or damage to an exhibit — relevant footage should be preserved until the matter is resolved, including any insurance claim or legal proceedings. Footage should be stored securely with access limited to authorised security personnel.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Is audio recording allowed in museum CCTV?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Audio recording in public museum and gallery spaces is generally not recommended. It is considered significantly more intrusive than video, and in practice is very difficult to justify under UK GDPR. The ICO advises that if audio is not essential to your security purpose, it should be disabled. Some venues use audio in specific high-security areas with prominent signage.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Do museums need CCTV signage?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes. Under UK GDPR Article 13, museums and galleries must inform visitors that CCTV is in operation. Signs must be clearly visible at all entrances, state the purpose of surveillance, identify the data controller (the museum trust or operating company), and provide contact details for privacy queries. The Surveillance Camera Code of Practice also recommends including information about how to request access to footage.

</div>
</details>
