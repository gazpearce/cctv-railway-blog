How to install CCTV in listed buildings and heritage sites without damaging historic fabric: conservation-compliant mounting, discreet camera options, and listed building consent procedures.

<hr class="faq-divider">

## Frequently Asked Questions

<div class="faq-list">

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Do I need planning permission for CCTV on a listed building?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes. Installing CCTV on a listed building almost always requires listed building consent from your local planning authority. External cameras can affect the building's character and appearance. Internal installations may also require consent if they involve drilling into historic fabric. Always consult your council's conservation officer before proceeding. Failing to obtain consent is a criminal offence.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>What type of CCTV is suitable for heritage sites?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Discreet, heritage-compliant cameras are essential for listed buildings. Options include: mini-dome cameras in heritage colours (black, bronze, or white rather than bright modern finishes), wireless cameras to minimise cabling damage to historic walls, cameras concealed within existing light fittings or architectural features, and conservation-grade mounting brackets that do not require drilling into stonework.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Can heritage sites use wireless CCTV?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Wireless CCTV is often preferred in heritage settings as it minimises the need for cables that would damage historic fabric. However, wireless signals in thick stone walls or lead-covered roofs may be unreliable. A site survey by a specialist heritage installer is recommended. If cabling is necessary, it should be run through existing conduit, under floorboards, or in discreet channels.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>What are the rules for CCTV in cathedral settings?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Cathedrals and major churches are often Grade I listed and subject to additional controls through the Cathedrals Fabric Commission for England. Any CCTV installation requires their approval. Cameras must be positioned to avoid detracting from architectural features and should be removable without leaving permanent marks. The Dean and Chapter must also ensure compliance with UK GDPR.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>How should heritage CCTV be signposted?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

CCTV signage in heritage settings should be sensitive to the environment. Modern plastic signs may not be appropriate in a historic context. Options include: discreet engraved brass or bronze plaques, signs integrated into existing information boards, or smaller-format signs in heritage-compatible materials. The ICO requires that signage be 'clearly visible' but does not prescribe specific materials.

</div>
</details>

<details class="faq-item">
<summary>Do heritage sites need ICO registration for CCTV?</summary>
<div class="faq-answer">

Yes. All heritage sites, including listed buildings open to the public, using CCTV that captures identifiable visitors must pay the ICO data protection fee. Church-owned sites may be eligible for reduced fees. Heritage organisations should also have a documented CCTV policy, display required signage, and respond to subject access requests within one calendar month.

</div>
</details>
