CCTV in Parks and Public Spaces — Community Safety Guide UK 2026

Guidance on deploying CCTV in UK parks and public spaces: reducing anti-social behaviour, the 12 guiding principles of the Surveillance Camera Code, and council compliance obligations.

By Gary Pearce May 18, 2026 3 min read

Guidance on deploying CCTV in UK parks and public spaces: reducing anti-social behaviour, the 12 guiding principles of the Surveillance Camera Code, and council compliance obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can councils install CCTV in public parks?

Yes, local authorities can install CCTV in public parks and open spaces, but they must comply with the Surveillance Camera Commissioner’s 12 guiding principles. These require that CCTV be used for a clearly stated purpose, be necessary and proportionate, have appropriate governance, and be subject to regular review. Councils must complete a DPIA before deployment and consult with the community.

What are the rules for CCTV signage in parks?

Park CCTV signage must be clearly visible at all main entrances and at intervals throughout the monitored area. Signs should include: the purpose of surveillance (e.g. ‘for your safety and to reduce anti-social behaviour’), the responsible authority (council or park trust), contact information, and details of how to access footage. Signs should be weatherproof and illuminated if the park is monitored at night.

How long should public space CCTV footage be kept?

Standard retention for public park CCTV is 30 days, consistent with ICO guidance. However, local authorities may adopt shorter retention periods — 14 to 21 days — for low-crime parks where extended retention is not justified. Retention policies must be documented in the council’s overarching CCTV policy and reviewed regularly. All systems should have automatic overwrite enabled.

Do parks need 'You Are Being Watched' signs?

The wording ‘CCTV in operation’ is preferred by the ICO over ‘You are being watched’, which can feel threatening. Park CCTV signage should be informative and proportionate. The Surveillance Camera Code of Practice recommends signs that explain the purpose of surveillance without causing unnecessary alarm. Signs should be designed to be accessible, including consideration for children and non-English speakers.

Can residents object to park CCTV?

Yes. Under UK GDPR Article 21, residents have the right to object to processing of their personal data for reasons relating to their particular situation. If a resident objects to park CCTV, the council must consider the objection and either stop processing or demonstrate compelling legitimate grounds. The ICO can also investigate complaints from residents who believe park CCTV is disproportionate.

How should park CCTV be reviewed?

The Surveillance Camera Code of Practice recommends that public space CCTV be reviewed annually. Reviews should assess: whether the CCTV is still necessary (crime data should support continued use), whether cameras are positioned correctly (vegetation growth may have created blind spots), whether image quality remains adequate, and whether technology upgrades are needed. Review findings should be published.
