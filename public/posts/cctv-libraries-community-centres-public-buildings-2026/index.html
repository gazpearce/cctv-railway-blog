CCTV for Libraries and Community Centres — Public Building Security UK 2026

How to implement proportionate CCTV in libraries and community centres: balancing public access, safeguarding vulnerable users, and meeting local authority data protection obligations.

By Gary Pearce May 18, 2026 3 min read

How to implement proportionate CCTV in libraries and community centres: balancing public access, safeguarding vulnerable users, and meeting local authority data protection obligations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should libraries have CCTV?

Many public libraries have CCTV to deter anti-social behaviour, protect staff and visitors, and safeguard library assets. However, the Surveillance Camera Commissioner’s code of practice emphasises that CCTV must be proportionate and necessary. Libraries should complete a DPIA before installation, clearly document their purpose, and consider less intrusive alternatives first.

Where should library cameras be placed?

Recommended camera locations include: main entrances and exits, reception/desk areas, computer areas (to protect equipment), and book stacks (for high-value or reference collections). Cameras must NOT be placed in toilet areas, private study rooms, or children’s reading corners where privacy expectations are higher. Fixed cameras are preferable to PTZ for public library settings.

Do community centres need CCTV signage?

Yes. All public buildings with CCTV must display clear signage at entrances stating that CCTV is in operation. Signs should include: the purpose of surveillance, the data controller’s identity (usually the local council or trust), contact information, and details of how to make a subject access request. Failure to display adequate signage can result in ICO enforcement action.

Can libraries record computer users' screens?

No. CCTV cameras should never be positioned to capture computer or device screens directly. This would capture sensitive personal data, potentially including financial information, health queries, or private communications. Screen recording would be a disproportionate intrusion and difficult to justify under UK GDPR. Libraries should use separate monitoring software if computer usage monitoring is needed.

How long can community centre CCTV footage be kept?

Standard retention is 30 days, in line with ICO guidance. Footage should be automatically overwritten after this period. If an incident is reported, relevant footage should be preserved until the investigation is concluded. Local authorities should have a documented CCTV policy specifying retention periods, access controls, and deletion procedures.

Can the public request library CCTV footage?

Yes. Any identifiable individual captured on library or community centre CCTV can make a subject access request under UK GDPR Article 15. The data controller (typically the local council) must respond within one calendar month. The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 allows councils to pause the clock while requesting additional information from the requester, such as specific dates and times.
